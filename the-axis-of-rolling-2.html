Feeling & Thought: The Axis of Rolling

Following my hot take on OSR and PbtA procedures I decided to think more about they ways we engage with dice. What follows is the Axis of Rolling.

This is a Feeling &amp; Thought. A series where I write about my experiences in the TTRPG hobby. Just like feelings, these thoughts aren't science, they aren't truth, and they definitely aren't reliable. Explore them with a playful attitude and don't take them too seriously. I certainly don't.

Definitions

Modern D&D - The style prevalent in D&D 5e, Pathfinder, Daggerheart, Draw Steel among others and popularized through actual play shows. Also called OC or neo-trad. Usually run by the Dungeon Master (DM).

PbtA - Powered by the Apocalypse. Games that follow the design conventions set by Apocalypse World. Generally characterized by story first play and genre emulation. Usually run by the Master of Ceremonies (MC).

OSR - The Old School Renaissance/Revival/Revolution. Games and playstyle that follows some desing conventions of the early editions of D&D. There is way too much lore you need to know to understand this philosophy well, so if you’re not familiar with the term I recommend looking into it. Usually run by the referee.

YZE - The Year Zero Engine. A (usually) dicepool game engine by Free League used in many of their games. Usually run by the Game Master (GM).

FitD - Forged in the Dark. Games that follow the design conventions set by Blades in the Dark. A dicepool game known for building on the ideas of PbtA and using the position and effect style for rolling, as well as a famous implementation of flasbacks.

Simple <-> Complex axis

This axis denotes the complexity of the rules procedure of a roll. Generally the d20 is often used as a very simple roll, and the outcome is almost as often read as a simple number the GM interprets through pre-set difficulties. The complex rolls involve dice-pools, tag-collecting or reading outcome lists, such as those found in many PbtA games.

Rarely <-> Often axis

The frequency of rolling is heavily dependent on the GM and the players. This axis I present as a throughline I observe in the ways people often play these games.

Rolling rarely is a common facet of the OSR, only using the dice when absolutely necessary. These games, procedure heavy or otherwise, see rolling as a necessary tool to enchance their fiction first gameplay.

Games that roll often usually follow the modern D&D tradition but this also encompasses games designed using the PbtA framework. This culture finds roling to be inherent to gameplay, sometimes lauded as the “push buttons on character sheet” style. However, there is nothing inherently wrong with wanting to roll dice and wanting to roll them often.

Modern D&D prefers to roll simple rolls but rolls often. The players ask for Checks to solve problems and the DM uses the roll as an oracular to gauge the direction of the story. It is very table dependent how direct the players are in asking for the Checks, many preferring to only describe the actions and the DM to ask for a roll.

PbtA follows the same logic of playing throuhg moves and rolling to see where the story goes. The difference  is that the game itself through more complex rolling procedure adjucates the outcome, as opposed to the DM in modern D&D style. Similar to modern D&D, PbtA games vary on how directly the players call out their moves.

The OSR wants as little as possible to do with rolling. The referee adjucates any and all situations unless a very specific situations requires a roll to resolve fairly. The dice systems are simple and quick to get back to the fiction. The players never ask for particular rolls.

Finally, the hardest quadrant to fill, I present the Year Zero Engine by Free League as an example of a complex roll done rarely. The YZE exists in an odd space between many traditions so I would not claim that it wants to roll particularly rarely like the OSR but personally I would want to run the game as such. The dicepool system promotes taking your time in considering your options and pooling in as many dice as possible for a single big roll. A similar style can be seen in FitD games, where negotiating for position and effect makes the roll complex enough that you really should not roll unless necessary to not bog down the game.

Conclusion

Let’s wrap this up with something useful: What are the quadrants good for. Here’s my take on the strenghts of each.

    quadrant-4 Impactful Moments

You could easily come up with counterexamples to each corner and argue the details. Rolling complex rolls often will make the crunchiest of combat games, and a storygame might not even have dice. This is not a taxonomy for games, merely my observation of how I feel some popular games fit into these axis. I invite you to play around with the Axis of Rolling and start placing games and / or definitions into the quadrants.
